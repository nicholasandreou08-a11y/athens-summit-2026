Locum's Nest in Numbers: The Story of Our Business Through Data

Presenter: Lara Day 1 | 09:30-10:30

Summary: A whole company view of the business through key metrics, telling the story of our impact, scale, and performance in an engaging, data-led way.

Full Details

This whole company session brings the Locum's Nest story to life through numbers. Using an engaging, presentation-led format, the session will walk through the key metrics that define how our platform, teams, and business performed, with a particular focus on 2025.

Data will be presented as a narrative rather than a report, helping everyone understand not just what the numbers are, but what they mean. The session will cover the full breadth of the business, from user and usage statistics to product, support, and company-wide metrics.

Along the way, interactive elements such as live polls or short quizzes will be used to keep energy high and encourage participation, reinforcing key insights in a memorable way.

The aim is to build shared understanding, pride, and context. By seeing the business through a single, coherent set of numbers, teams can better appreciate scale, impact, and progress, and understand how their work contributes to the bigger picture.

Expected Outcomes

  • A shared, data-grounded view of the business
  • Greater clarity on scale, performance, and momentum
  • Increased engagement through interactive storytelling
  • Stronger connection between individual teams and company-wide impact

Product Direction and Growth Opportunities in 2026

Presenters: Nick and Ahmed Day 1 | 10:45-11:30

Summary: A founder-led overview of where we are heading in 2026, outlining our product direction, key growth opportunities, and the priorities that will guide the year ahead.

Full Details

This session sets out the product and growth direction for Locum's Nest in 2026. Delivered by the founders, it will connect our mission, market position and long-term ambition with the concrete areas where we will focus our effort over the next year.

The session will look at where we see the biggest opportunities for growth across product, customers and partnerships, how the market is evolving, and what this means for our priorities.

It will also be clear about what is not in scope, helping everyone understand the choices we are making and why focus matters.

Under the Hoot: Understanding the Platform

Facilitators: Sue & Ben, Ellie & Rami, Vasilis & Aris Day 1 | 11:30-13:30

Summary: Three parallel deep dives that build platform fluency, from the end-to-end customer journey to advanced features and future data possibilities.

Full Details

These parallel In-Depth Knowledge Transfer sessions are designed to build shared understanding of the Locum's Nest platform at different levels of depth. Together, they cover the full customer journey, the detail behind our newest and most complex capabilities, and the data and reporting that underpin decision making.

The sessions aim to strengthen product fluency across the organisation, align how we talk about the platform with customers, and spark informed thinking about what we can build next.

Track A: End to End Platform Walkthrough

Facilitators: Sue and Ben

This session is designed primarily for newer joiners. Participants will experience a full, end-to-end walkthrough of the Locum's Nest platform from a customer-facing perspective, following the typical journey and interactions of our users.

Colleagues from non-front-line teams will also be invited to observe and participate, helping them build familiarity with customer language, tone, and expectations. The session will emphasise how features are explained and demonstrated in practice, providing useful context for teams who design, build, or support the product.

Track B: Deep Dive into 2025 Releases and Advanced Features

Facilitators: Ellie and Rami

This session is aimed at more experienced internal users. It will focus on a walkthrough of key 2025 releases, alongside some of our more complex or newer capabilities, including cost-saving features, control mechanisms, and exception reporting.

Beyond understanding functionality, the session will create space to discuss how these features should be positioned, explained, and framed when engaging with end users, ensuring clarity, consistency, and confidence in how we talk about our more advanced products.

Track C: Data, Reporting, and What Is Possible

Facilitators: Vasilis and Aris

This session will explore the data and reporting capabilities available within Locum's Nest, examining how reports are currently used internally and with customers.

The discussion will be deliberately exploratory, highlighting existing value while encouraging participants to think creatively about future opportunities and unmet needs. Representatives from the technical teams will join to share context on how data is generated, structured, and constrained, helping bridge understanding between what exists today and what could realistically be developed next.

From Ideation to Iteration: Product Development Learnings

Facilitator: Evripides Day 1 | 14:30-16:30

Summary: A reflective product session focused on learning what works, what does not, and how we deliver, release, market, sell, and evolve software effectively.

Full Details

This workstream focuses on shared learning across the full product development lifecycle, from early ideation and discovery through to delivery, release, adoption and iteration.

The session is not intended to design new processes on the spot, but to surface practical insights from real experience, both what has worked well and where teams have struggled. Participants will reflect on recent initiatives and releases, examining how ideas were generated, shaped, prioritised, scoped, built, marketed/sold and evolved once in the hands of users.

Particular attention will be paid to handovers between stages, decision-making moments, and feedback loops, including how learning from customers and internal teams feeds back into future iterations.

The discussion will aim to create a shared understanding of effective behaviours, tools, and ways of working across product, engineering, design, and implementation teams.

Session Structure

Section 1 (50 mins): How to deliver and adopt software effectively, with agility and impact, in an environment where not everything is known upfront and change is constant. At what point should the client-facing, marketing and sales teams be involved?

Section 2 (50 mins): How to ensure that the pace of product releases is aligned with user expectations and adoption, while accounting for challenges such as change management dynamics and the capacity of teams and users to absorb new functionality.

Section 3 (20 mins): A discussion on the shared learnings between Section 1 and 2.

Expected Outputs

  • A shared view of what works well across ideation, delivery, adoption and iteration
  • Key challenges and failure points observed in recent product work
  • Agreed learnings and principles to carry forward into future initiatives and agreed areas to keep exploring in further workshops

Discover Athens

Everything you need to know about the birthplace of democracy

History of Athens

Athens, named after the goddess Athena, is one of the world's oldest cities with a recorded history spanning over 3,400 years. It is widely regarded as the cradle of Western civilisation and the birthplace of democracy.

Ancient Glory (5th Century BC)

The "Golden Age of Athens" under Pericles saw the construction of the Parthenon and the flourishing of arts, philosophy, and democracy. This era produced Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, Sophocles, and Euripides.

The Birthplace of Democracy

Around 508 BC, Cleisthenes introduced demokratia - "rule by the people." Citizens gathered on the Pnyx hill to debate and vote on laws, establishing principles that still guide governments today.

Philosophy & Learning

Athens was home to Plato's Academy (387 BC) and Aristotle's Lyceum - the first institutions of higher learning in the Western world. These schools shaped Western thought for millennia.

Modern Athens

After centuries under Roman, Byzantine, and Ottoman rule, Athens became the capital of independent Greece in 1834. Today it's a vibrant metropolis of 3.7 million people, blending ancient monuments with contemporary culture.

Did you know? The Acropolis has been a place of worship for over 5,000 years - from prehistoric times through ancient Greek religion to a Christian church and an Ottoman mosque.

A Day in the Life of an Ancient Athenian

Dawn Rising & Morning Rituals

You wake at first light in your modest home near the Agora. After a simple breakfast of barley bread dipped in wine, you pour a small libation to Zeus Herkeios, protector of the household. Your wife and children remain in the women's quarters - the gynaeceum - as you prepare for your day in public life.

Morning The Agora Awakens

The Agora buzzes with activity. You browse the stalls - pottery from Corinth, fish from the Piraeus, olives from Attica. But the real business is conversation. You debate yesterday's Assembly vote with a neighbor, hear the latest news from a merchant just arrived from Ionia, and catch a glimpse of Socrates interrogating yet another confident young man about the nature of justice.

Midday The Gymnasium

You strip naked (gymnos - hence "gymnasium") and oil your body for exercise. Wrestling, running, discus - physical excellence is as important as mental. Afterwards, in the shade of the colonnade, a philosopher lectures on the movement of the stars. Body and mind, both cultivated. This is what it means to be a free Greek.

Afternoon Civic Duty

Today your name was drawn for jury duty - 6,000 citizens serve each year. You sit with 500 others judging a case of impiety. The accused claims he merely questioned the old stories about the gods. The vote is close. Democracy is exhausting work, but you wouldn't trade it for Persian gold.

Evening The Symposium

Tonight you're invited to a symposium - a drinking party, but far more. Reclining on couches, you mix wine with water (only barbarians drink it neat). Between cups, guests recite poetry, debate philosophy, play drinking games. A flute-girl plays in the corner. The conversation turns to love, then politics, then love of politics. You stumble home past midnight, your head full of wine and ideas.

Dawn The Gynaeceum

Your world is the women's quarters - the gynaeceum at the back of the house. You wake surrounded by your children and female slaves. Respectable women rarely leave home except for religious festivals. Your husband has already left; you may not see him until evening.

Morning Managing the Household

You are the oikodespina - mistress of the household. You distribute the day's rations to slaves, inspect the weaving, ensure the children are fed. Your domain may be confined, but within it, you hold considerable power. You manage the finances, the stores, the servants. A poorly-run household brings shame to everyone.

Midday Weaving & Work

The loom dominates the main room. Weaving is women's work - sacred, even. Athena herself is goddess of the craft. You weave cloth for your family, but also for religious offerings. The peplos presented to Athena at the Panathenaia is woven by chosen women and girls. In thread, you have a voice.

Afternoon The Thesmophoria

But today is different - it's the festival of Thesmophoria, sacred to Demeter. For three days, only married women participate. Your husband is forbidden to know what occurs. You process to the sanctuary with other wives, perform secret rites for fertility and the harvest. In religion, women have mysteries that men can never know.

Evening Shadows & Stories

While your husband reclines at his symposium, you eat separately with the children and slaves. You tell them stories - of Odysseus and Penelope, of Helen and Troy. Your daughter asks why Penelope had to wait twenty years. You have no good answer, only the hope that her future husband will be kind.

Dawn The Outsider's Morning

You came from Miletus fifteen years ago, fleeing the Persian advance. Athens welcomed you - for a price. As a metic, a resident alien, you pay a special tax (metoikion) each month. You can never own land, never vote, never hold office. But you can work, trade, and grow rich. Many do.

Morning The Workshop

Your pottery workshop in the Kerameikos employs twelve men - slaves and free workers alike. Athenian citizens consider trade beneath them; their loss is your gain. Your red-figure vases are famous from Sicily to the Black Sea. An Athenian aristocrat wouldn't shake your hand, but he'll pay handsomely for your work.

Midday Banking & Business

You meet your banker, Pasion - himself a former slave who bought his freedom and became one of the richest men in Athens. He's now a citizen, granted the privilege for his services to the state. Perhaps one day, if you're useful enough, they'll grant you citizenship too. Perhaps.

Afternoon The Price of Protection

You need a citizen sponsor (prostates) for any legal business. Today, you're in court defending a contract dispute. You cannot speak - your prostates speaks for you. If war comes, you'll be required to serve in the army, to pay war taxes, to die for Athens. But you cannot vote for war or peace. Taxation without representation, you think, but keep it to yourself.

Evening Between Two Worlds

You dine with other metics - a Phoenician shipowner, a Thracian armorer, a Syrian banker. You speak Greek, worship Greek gods, raise your children as Greeks. Your grandchildren will likely be the same - permanent outsiders in the city you've built. "We are the ones who make the wheels turn," says the Phoenician. "They just take the credit."

Dawn Before the Sun

You rise before the family - there is water to fetch, fires to light, bread to bake. You were born in Thrace; captured in a raid when you were eight. That was twenty years ago. You barely remember your mother's face, only that she screamed when they took you. Your name then was different. Now you're called Syros - "the Syrian" - though you've never been to Syria.

Morning Work Without End

Your master is a craftsman - better than the silver mines, where slaves die within years. You've learned his trade, manage his accounts, bargain with suppliers. He trusts you, as much as any master trusts a slave. Sometimes he almost forgets what you are. You never forget.

Midday The Mask of Servitude

At the market, a philosopher argues that some men are "natural slaves." You keep your face blank - the essential skill. Inside, you think of your father, a free warrior. You think of what you would do to this philosopher if you could. The thoughts sustain you, even as you bow your head.

Afternoon A Peculiar Hope

You've saved money - your master allows you to keep a portion of tips from customers. In ten more years, maybe fifteen, you might buy your freedom. Some do. Your friend Pasion was once a slave banker; now he's one of the richest men in Athens, a citizen even. But for every Pasion, there are ten thousand who die in chains. You try not to think about the odds.

Evening Dreams of the Free

In your small room behind the workshop, you listen to the city grow quiet. Somewhere, citizens are drinking and philosophizing about the good life. Tomorrow, as today, you will rise before dawn. But sometimes in dreams, you run through Thracian forests, and your name is your own again. One-third of Athens' population lives like you - invisible, essential, unfree. The birthplace of democracy was built on your back.

Key Monuments In-Depth

The Parthenon

447-432 BC â€¢ Temple of Athena Parthenos

The Parthenon wasn't just a temple - it was a statement. After the Persians burned the Acropolis in 480 BC, Athens rebuilt bigger, bolder, using money from the Delian League (their "allies" might say extorted). The architect Iktinos incorporated subtle optical refinements: columns that lean inward, a floor that curves upward, all to counteract visual distortions and make the building appear perfectly straight.

Inside stood Pheidias' masterpiece - a 12-meter statue of Athena made of gold and ivory worth more than the entire building. It's long gone, melted down or destroyed. The outer frieze depicted the Panathenaic procession; the metopes showed Greeks battling Amazons, gods fighting giants - civilization versus chaos, order versus barbarism.

The Controversy: Half the Parthenon's surviving sculptures sit in the British Museum - the "Elgin Marbles." Lord Elgin removed them in 1801-1812 when Greece was under Ottoman rule. Greece has demanded their return for decades. Britain argues they're better preserved in London. The debate embodies colonial legacy and cultural ownership questions that remain unresolved.

The Erechtheion & Caryatids

421-406 BC â€¢ Sacred to multiple gods

The Erechtheion is weird - intentionally. It sits on uneven ground where Athens' most sacred relics competed for space: the olive tree Athena created to win patronage of the city, the salt spring Poseidon struck with his trident, the tomb of the legendary King Erechtheus. The architects couldn't move any of it, so they built around it all, creating an asymmetrical masterpiece.

The six maidens of the south porch - the Caryatids - are architectural sculpture at its finest. They're not just decorative columns; they're load-bearing structures, each slightly different, their draped robes concealing the engineering that lets them hold up the roof. Why women? Perhaps they represent the women of Karyai, enslaved for siding with Persia. Or perhaps they're priestesses, eternally serving the goddess.

Where are they now? Five of the original six Caryatids are in the Acropolis Museum; one is in the British Museum (taken by Elgin). The ones you see on the temple are replicas. When the originals were moved, conservators found that their faces had been eroded by Athens' pollution - they were literally weeping acid tears.

Theatre of Dionysus

6th century BC â€¢ Birthplace of drama

Theatre was invented here. Not entertainment - religion. The performances were sacred to Dionysus, god of wine and ecstasy. Attending was a civic duty; the poor received state subsidies for tickets. At its peak, 17,000 Athenians would sit here, watching their myths transformed, their assumptions questioned, their certainties shattered.

This is where Aeschylus staged the Oresteia, wrestling with justice and revenge. Where Sophocles' Oedipus discovered the terrible truth. Where Euripides made audiences weep for Medea, a foreign woman driven to infanticide. Where Aristophanes mocked politicians by name, put Socrates in a basket suspended from the ceiling, and imagined women seizing power through a sex strike.

Method acting, ancient-style: All roles were played by men wearing masks. The acoustics were so precise that a coin dropped on the orchestra floor could be heard in the back row. The front-row marble seats (still visible) were reserved for priests and dignitaries - ancient VIP seating.

The Ancient Agora

6th century BC â€¢ Heart of democracy

Forget the Acropolis - this is where Athenian life actually happened. The Agora was marketplace, meeting place, and civic center rolled into one. Here citizens debated, voted, shopped, gossiped, and philosophized. Socrates spent his days here, questioning everyone from generals to shoemakers. It was also where they eventually condemned him to death.

The Stoa of Attalos (now reconstructed) gives you a sense of the colonnade-lined streets where Athenians escaped the Mediterranean sun. The Tholos was where 50 representatives ate and slept, always ready for emergencies. The Bouleuterion held the council of 500. And scattered throughout were the small shops and workshops that kept the democracy fed.

Ostracism: Once a year, citizens could vote to exile anyone who was getting too powerful. They scratched the name on pottery shards (ostraka) - thousands have been found in the Agora. If 6,000 voted against you, you had ten days to leave Athens for ten years. Democracy's reset button.

Temple of Olympian Zeus

174 BC - 131 AD â€¢ 700 years to build

Some projects have scope creep. This one had scope gallop. Started by the tyrant Peisistratos around 515 BC, abandoned when democracy came, resumed by a Hellenistic king in 174 BC, finally finished by the Roman Emperor Hadrian in 131 AD. That's over 600 years of construction - longer than the time from Shakespeare to today.

When complete, it was one of the largest temples in the ancient world: 104 Corinthian columns, each 17 meters tall. Inside sat a massive gold and ivory statue of Zeus, with a smaller one of Hadrian beside it (subtle, Hadrian). Only 15 columns remain standing; a 16th lies where it fell in a storm in 1852. The rest were quarried for building material over centuries.

Hadrian's ego: Hadrian loved Athens. He finished the temple, built a library, and constructed an arch (still standing) with an inscription: one side reads "This is Athens, the ancient city of Theseus," the other "This is the city of Hadrian, not of Theseus." Not exactly humble.

Modern Athens: From Revolution to Rebirth

1821
War of Independence
Greece rises against 400 years of Ottoman rule

On March 25, 1821, Bishop Germanos raised the flag of revolution at Agia Lavra monastery. The Greek War of Independence captivated European romantics - Lord Byron died at Missolonghi fighting for the cause. Athens changed hands multiple times; the Acropolis was besieged twice. By 1832, with help from Britain, France, and Russia, Greece was free - but Athens lay in ruins, its population barely 4,000.

1834
Athens Becomes Capital
A village transforms into a nation's heart

When Athens was named capital, it was a dusty village of goats and ruins. King Otto (a Bavarian teenager imposed by the Great Powers) brought German architects who laid out a neoclassical city. The "Old Palace" (now Parliament) rose on Syntagma Square. Grand buildings evoked ancient glory, papering over the awkward truth that modern Greece was a European invention as much as a Greek one.

1896
First Modern Olympics
Ancient games reborn in their homeland

Baron Pierre de Coubertin's dream was realized in the rebuilt Panathenaic Stadium - the only stadium in the world made entirely of marble. Greece was nearly bankrupt, but a wealthy benefactor funded the games. 14 nations participated. A Greek shepherd named Spyridon Louis won the marathon (a race invented for these Olympics), becoming an instant national hero.

1941-1944
Nazi Occupation
Resistance and famine under the swastika

When Germany invaded, the Nazis raised their flag on the Acropolis. Two young men, Manolis Glezos and Apostolos Santas, tore it down in one of the first acts of resistance in occupied Europe. The occupation brought devastating famine - over 300,000 Greeks died in Athens alone. Yet resistance flourished, and Greece tied down Axis divisions that might have fought elsewhere.

1967-1974
Military Junta
The colonels seize power, students resist

A military coup suspended democracy. The junta imprisoned opponents, censored the press, and banned long hair and miniskirts. In November 1973, students occupied the Athens Polytechnic. Tanks crashed through the gates; dozens died. The massacre galvanized opposition. When the junta's Cyprus adventure provoked a Turkish invasion, the colonels collapsed. November 17 remains a day of protest marches past the US Embassy (America supported the junta).

2004
Olympic Transformation
A city reinvents itself for the world stage

The 2004 Olympics were Athens' coming-out party after decades of chaos and pollution. A new airport, a metro system (yielding thousands of archaeological finds), pedestrianized streets around the Acropolis, the stunning new Acropolis Museum - the city transformed. The opening ceremony featured a glowing Cycladic figure rising from a pool, connecting ancient and modern. The debt from these games, however, contributed to crises to come.

2010s
Crisis and Resilience
Economic collapse spawns creative rebirth

The debt crisis devastated Greece. Unemployment hit 27%; youth unemployment 60%. Athens saw protests, riots, shuttered shops. But something else emerged: a remarkable creative resilience. Street art transformed grey walls into galleries. Abandoned buildings became cultural centers. Young entrepreneurs created from necessity. The crisis generation reinvented what it meant to be Athenian.

Today
Athens Now
3.7 million people, ancient and contemporary

Modern Athens is chaotic, loud, graffitied, and alive. It's a city where you can see 2,500-year-old temples from your apartment balcony, where stray dogs have their own metro passes, where dinner doesn't start until 10 PM, where a conversation with a taxi driver might cover philosophy, football, and the failings of government - often simultaneously. It's not a museum. It's a living argument with its own past.
